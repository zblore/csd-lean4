                                              ABSTRACT

LF3 formalises in Lean4 the finite-dimensional singlet-kernel and projective pointer-sector decom-
position used in the Bell-singlet measurement layer of Constraint-Surface Dynamics. It is the third
layer of the CSD formalisation programme. LF1 formalises repeated-trial frequency convergence,
LF2 formalises the measure bridge and Born-weight wrapper, and LF3 applies the LF2 operator-form
weight interface to a finite-dimensional projective readout model for the two-qubit singlet sector.

The purpose of LF3 is not to rederive typicality, the Fubini¡Study measure bridge, or the Born-
form probability assignment. Nor does LF3 derive the pointer-readout dynamics from a concrete
microscopic finite-dimensional pointer generator. Instead, the Hamiltonian-control and branch-
separation layers are encoded as explicit structure-field interfaces. Against those interfaces, LF3
verifies the projective pointer-sector decomposition, the branch-weight formula, the singlet probability
kernel, the correlation identity, the marginal identities, and structural finite-leakage stability.

The formalisation is carried out for a two-qubit system coupled to two finite-dimensional pointer
sectors. The joint system¡apparatus Hilbert space is

               HS(NA) = C2  C2  KA(dA)  KB(dB),                       N = 4dAdB.

For detector settings a, b  S2, the pointer-sector readout is represented by four projectors

               Ms(ta,b) = IAB  QsA  QBt ,                      s, t  {▒1}.

LF3 verifies that these projectors form a complete orthogonal projective readout and that, when applied
to the branch-separated singlet-pointer state supplied by the structural interface, their operator-form
weights reproduce the singlet kernel.

LF3 also formalises structural finite-leakage control. The parameters A and B are treated as explicit
pointer-readout compatibility bounds. They encode the compatibility between the selected pointer

generators, translated pointer states, and readout subspaces. They are not derived in LF3 from a
concrete microscopic pointer construction. Under these structural bounds,

                 Pst(a, b; T ) -  1 - st a À b       A + B + AB.

                 |ET (a, b) + a À b|  4(A + B + AB),

with the analogous bound for PB.

LF3 therefore supplies the machine-checked singlet-kernel and projective pointer-sector layer of the
CSD formalisation branch. Its scope is deliberately restricted. It does not formalise the Ashtekar¡
Schilling correspondence, the full ontic basin dynamics on , general POVMs, mixed states, sub-
system reduction, arbitrary multipartite Bell scenarios, or a derivation of small leakage from an
explicit finite pointer model. Within its finite-dimensional singlet setting, LF3 establishes a complete
theorem package for projective readout, singlet correlations, no-signalling marginals, structural
leakage stability, and context-indexed outcome maps.

1.2 Position within the Lean Formalisation Programme

The Lean formalisation branch of Constraint-Surface Dynamics is organised as a sequence of narrow theorem layers
rather than as a single monolithic reconstruction. LF1 formalises the repeated-trial theorem underlying volume typicality.
Its role is to show that, under a repeated-preparation model, empirical frequencies converge to normalised ontic weights.
LF1 is therefore a frequency theorem, not a Born-rule theorem.

LF2 formalises the next layer. It isolates the finite-dimensional measure-and-weight interface between deterministic
volume typicality and operational quantum weights. In particular, LF2 treats the pushforward from ontic Liouville
measure to projective-state weights and packages the Born-weight layer using explicitly stated external theorem inputs.
LF2 is therefore the appropriate upstream dependency for LF3: it supplies the formal probability interface that LF3
uses when assigning weights to projective pointer-sector outcomes.

LF3 occupies the next position in this sequence:

                                            LF1 - LF2 - LF3 - LF4 - LF5.

Within this ordering, LF3 is the first formalisation layer concerned with an explicit system-apparatus measurement
model. It does not generalise the operational dictionary to mixed states, POVMs, or subsystem reduction. Those topics
belong to LF4. It also does not formalise outcome-conditioned update or sequential experiments. Those topics belong
to LF5. LF3 is instead the projective-readout layer: it shows how the finite-dimensional Born-weight interface is
instantiated in a concrete pointer-sector measurement for the Bell-singlet case.

This division of labour is essential. If LF3 attempted simultaneously to formalise typicality, the measure bridge,
Born-weight uniqueness, pointer dynamics, mixed states, POVMs, and sequential update, the result would obscure the
logical dependencies of the programme. The aim of LF3 is more disciplined: to make the TN3 constructive singlet
layer machine-checkable while preserving the upstream and downstream theorem boundaries.

Hamiltonian, derive the Born rule, or solve the general measurement problem. Instead, it supplies the explicit

1.5 Scope of the Formalisation

The scope of LF3 is finite-dimensional, projective, and singlet-specific. The formalisation is intentionally restricted to
the two-qubit Bell-singlet measurement with finite-dimensional pointer sectors. This restriction is not a weakness of the
paper. It is the reason the formalisation is well-posed and achievable.

the CSD ontic picture, but the Lean target is the finite-dimensional system-apparatus readout structure, not a complete
classification of ontic basins or physically realised measurement partitions.

operational machinery developed in TN4 and are assigned to LF4.

LF3 also does not prove a general theorem about all Bell scenarios. It treats the two-qubit singlet as the canonical finite-
dimensional case in which the pointer-sector construction can be made explicit. The broader no-go-theorem analysis is
supplied by companion Fine-Bell work and is used here only to state the correct boundary between context-indexed
deterministic outcomes and forbidden global joint assignments.

3 Measurement Hamiltonian and Factorised Evolution

3.1 Purpose of the Hamiltonian Layer

This section defines the measurement Hamiltonian used in LF3 and records the algebraic facts needed by the later
branch-separation and pointer-sector arguments. The construction is the finite-dimensional version of the standard
von Neumann pointer coupling. It is not introduced as a new physical Hamiltonian. Its role is to give a precise
system-apparatus control model for the Bell-singlet measurement in the finite-dimensional setting.

The central object is the setting-dependent readout Hamiltonian

                                                       Ha,b = HA(a) + HB(b),

acting on
                                                  HS(NA) = HAB  KA(dA)  KB(dB).

The two terms represent the two local readout couplings. The A-term couples the A-qubit spin observable to the
A-pointer. The B-term couples the B-qubit spin observable to the B-pointer. Since the two couplings act on distinct
tensor factors, they commute. This commutation is the algebraic reason the measurement unitary factorises.

3.2 The Impulsive Readout Idealisation

LF3 uses the impulsive-readout idealisation. During the measurement interval, the free Hamiltonian of the system and
the free Hamiltonians of the pointers are neglected relative to the readout coupling. Thus the primary derivation sets

                                           Hfree = 0

and uses only the interaction Hamiltonian

                                                       Ha,b = HA(a) + HB(b).

This is a standard approximation in von Neumann measurement models. It isolates the readout step and allows the
pointer translation induced by the measurement coupling to be treated directly.

The approximation is not a claim that free evolution is physically absent. It is a controlled modelling choice for the
readout interval. Corrections due to finite readout time, imperfect localisation, and finite pointer separation are treated
later through the leakage parameters

                                                               A, B.

The readout Hamiltonian must be self-adjoint so that it generates unitary evolution. The Pauli-direction operators are

The two readout terms commute:

Equivalently,                              HA(a)HB(b) = HB(b)HA(a).

4 Branch Separation and Finite-Leakage Bounds

4.1 Purpose of the Branch-Separation Layer

This section records the branch-separation structure produced by the finite-dimensional pointer measurement. The
previous section defined the setting-dependent measurement Hamiltonian and its factorised unitary evolution. The
present section applies that unitary to the initial singlet-pointer state and obtains the branch-separated form used in the
later pointer-sector probability calculation.

The key point is that the measurement interaction correlates each system eigenbranch with a corresponding pair of
pointer states. In the ideal strong-readout limit, these pointer states lie entirely inside disjoint readout sectors. At finite
pointer separation, however, each translated pointer state may have a small overlap with the wrong readout sector. LF3
therefore includes explicit leakage parameters

                                                               A, B,

and proves that branch distinguishability and branch weights are stable up to errors of order

                                                               O(A + B).

This layer is essential because it prevents the formalisation from relying only on exact mathematical separation.
The ideal projective measurement is recovered as a limiting case, while the finite-dimensional construction remains
meaningful when pointer separation is large but not perfect.

4.7 Asymptotic Branch-Separation Lemma

                                                                 A + B.

4.10 Interpretation of the Leakage Bounds

The leakage parameters do not modify the singlet probability law. They quantify imperfections in the pointer readout
stage. In the exact finite-dimensional construction, they record how well the translated pointer states are resolved by the
chosen readout subspaces.

The finite-leakage estimate has three roles in LF3.

First, it separates the algebraic singlet calculation from the physical readout approximation. The coefficients

                                                                  cst(a, b)

belong to the system-side singlet expansion, while

                                                                A, B

belong to the apparatus-side readout.

Second, it makes the strong-readout limit explicit:
                                                      Pst(a, b; T )  |cst(a, b)|2.

This is the point at which the later singlet calculation becomes the operational pointer-sector probability.

Third, it shows stability. Small imperfections in pointer separation cause only small deviations in the branch weights.
This is essential for interpreting the construction as a finite-dimensional approximation to the ideal projective measure-
ment.

5.12 No Use of Fubini-Study Voronoi Volumes

No Fubini-Study Voronoi-volume construction is used in LF3. The outcome sectors are not defined as nearest-ray cells
in projective space. They are defined by physical pointer readout projectors:

                                 Ms(ta,b) = IAB  QAs  QtB .

Their weights are computed by

                                 Pst(a, b; T ) = T |Ms(ta,b)|T .

The Fubini-Study measure remains part of the upstream measure bridge in the CSD programme, but it is not the
operational mechanism used to compute the pointer-sector weights in this formalisation layer.

This distinction prevents a common misunderstanding. LF3 is not claiming that the Bell-singlet branch weights are
obtained by measuring the Fubini-Study volumes of four projective Voronoi cells. Rather, LF3 formalises a standard
finite-dimensional projective measurement on the system-apparatus Hilbert space and then uses the LF2 operator-form
weight rule.

8.5 Absence of Bell-Local Factorisation

The pointer-sector construction gives deterministic readout structure for each realised measurement context. It does not

                                                   F (a,b) : Da,b  {▒1} Î {▒1}.

                                                      Ha,b.

8.7 No Global Joint Assignment Across Incompatible Contexts

In a CHSH setting, each side has two possible detector settings:

                                                          a1, a2 and b1, b2.

A global joint assignment would require a single probability space supporting four simultaneously defined random
variables:

                                            A(a1), A(a2), B(b1), B(b2).
Equivalently, one would require a single measurable map

                                                            G :   {▒1}4,

with
                                        G() = (A(a1, ), A(a2, ), B(b1, ), B(b2, )) .

LF3 does not construct such a map. Instead, it constructs context-specific maps:

                       F (ai,bj) : Dai,bj  {▒1} Î {▒1},                            i, j  {1, 2}.

                       (a1, b1), (a1, b2), (a2, b1), (a2, b2).

The programme does not identify these four context maps as marginals of one global joint assignment.

This is the structural point required by Fine-Bell consistency. Fine's theorem shows that, in the CHSH scenario, the
existence of a global joint distribution for all four incompatible observables is equivalent to satisfaction of the Bell
inequalities. Since the singlet probabilities violate those inequalities for suitable settings, LF3 must not imply such a
global joint distribution.

8.8 Contextual Determinism
The correct LF3 statement is contextual determinism:

                               (a, b), the realised context has a well-defined outcome sector.
It is not global value determinism:

                            G assigning outcomes for all incompatible settings simultaneously.

At the pointer level, contextual determinism is represented by the four-sector projective decomposition associated with

the realised context:

                       HS(NA) =                                       R(sat ,b) .

                                                             s,t{▒1}

                       F (a,b) = (s, t).

However, there is no corresponding statement that the same physical run also has definite values for the unperformed
contexts

                                                   (a, b), (a, b), (a, b).

This is not an ad hoc escape from Bell's theorem. It is the standard distinction between:

1. a deterministic outcome for the measurement actually performed;
2. a joint assignment of outcomes to all measurements that could have been performed.

LF3 formalises the first structure in the two-qubit pointer model. It does not assume the second structure.

9 Lean4 Formalisation Architecture

9.1 Purpose of the Formalisation Architecture

This section records the intended Lean4 structure for LF3. The purpose is not to give final Lean source code, but to
specify the repository architecture, module dependencies, theorem targets, and assumption boundaries required for
implementation.

LF3 is designed as a narrow formalisation layer. Its task is to verify the finite-dimensional quantum-pointer construction
for the Bell-singlet measurement. The implementation should therefore avoid unnecessary generality. It should not
attempt to formalise all of geometric quantum mechanics, all of CSD, or all of Bell theory. The correct implementation
target is the finite-dimensional chain:

9.3 Implementation Principles

The implementation should follow five principles.

First, LF3 should remain finite-dimensional. All Hilbert spaces, pointer sectors, operators, and projectors should be
encoded in finite-dimensional form. This avoids unbounded-operator theory and continuous spectral analysis.

Second, LF3 should use abstract structures where Mathlib support is incomplete. For example, if finite-dimensional
tensor-product operator identities are expensive to prove directly, the relevant algebraic properties may initially be
packaged as structure fields or local lemmas, then discharged later.

Third, LF3 should separate exact strong-readout results from finite-leakage estimates. The exact theorem

                                       Pst(a, b) =  1 - st a À b

                                                                 4

                                                   51
LF3: Machine-Verified Singlet Kernel and Projective Pointer-Sector Decomposition with Structural LeakaAgePRBEoPuRnINdsT

should be proved independently of finite-leakage bounds. The finite-leakage theorem should then wrap the exact result
with explicit error estimates.

Fourth, LF3 should make all imported assumptions visible. In particular, the LF2 Born-weight wrapper should be
referenced as an upstream interface rather than reproved.

Fifth, LF3 should avoid proving Fine's theorem unless the required infrastructure is already available. The no-global-
assignment statement may be represented as an imported theorem or an explicit axiom boundary.

10.2 Relation to Paper B

Paper B supplies the finite-dimensional Born-weight layer. Its purpose is to fix the admissible probability assignment
on finite-dimensional projective state space using symmetry and operational consistency. LF3 does not repeat this
argument.

In LF3, the relevant probability rule is used in operator form:

                                              w(P | ) = |P |.

For the pointer-sector projectors,

                                                     P = Ms(ta,b),

and for the final system-apparatus state,

                                                     | = |T ,

this gives

                                              Pst(a, b; T ) = T |Ms(ta,b)|T .

map of the formalisation without requiring the main text to carry all repository-level details.
The theorem map follows the LF3 module structure:

  Setup  Hamiltonian  BranchSeparation  Projectors  Singlet  ContextMap  Interface.

Each group below identifies the intended mathematical content of the corresponding Lean file. The declarations are
schematic. They are not final Lean syntax. Their role is to define the target statements that the implementation should
realise.

B Finite-Leakage Estimate Details

B.1 Purpose of this Appendix

This appendix records the finite-leakage estimates used in LF3. The purpose is to separate the ideal strong-readout
calculation from the finite-separation error analysis.
In the ideal strong-readout limit, each translated pointer state lies entirely inside its intended readout sector. In that case,

                                                        Pst(a, b) = |cst(a, b)|2.
At finite pointer separation, each translated pointer state may have a small component in the wrong readout sector. LF3
records this by introducing leakage parameters

                                                               A, B.
The goal is to show that finite pointer leakage perturbs the branch weights, correlation, and marginals by controlled
amounts.
The estimates in this appendix use only:

        À orthogonality and completeness of the pointer readout projectors;
        À the branch-separated final state;
        À non-negativity of pointer-sector overlaps;
        À the leakage bounds defining A and B;
        À finite summation over s, t  {▒1}.

No continuum pointer analysis is required.

C Repository Implementation Order

C.1 Purpose of this Appendix

This appendix gives the recommended implementation order for LF3 in the Lean repository. The purpose is practical.
The paper-level theorem structure is already defined in the main text and Appendix A. The present appendix translates
that structure into a staged build plan.

The recommended order is:
         Setup.lean  Projectors.lean  Singlet.lean  ContextMap.lean  Interface.lean

with
                                    Hamiltonian.lean and BranchSeparation.lean

introduced either before or after the first algebraic singlet theorem, depending on implementation difficulty.

This ordering is slightly different from the conceptual order of the paper. The reason is that the fastest route to a
compiling LF3 theorem package is to prove the algebraic projective-readout and singlet results first, then enrich the
formalisation with the Hamiltonian and leakage layers.

C.7 Implementation Stage 2: Pointer Projectors

The second implementation stage should introduce the pointer-sector projectors

                                                     Ms(ta,b) = IAB  QsA  QBt .

The immediate target is to prove that these projectors form a complete orthogonal readout.

If constructing the projectors from subspaces is difficult in the first pass, define an abstract structure:

structure FourOutcomeProjectiveReadout where
   M : Sign -> Sign -> Operator H
   idem :
      forall s t, M s t * M s t = M s t
   selfAdjoint :
      forall s t, IsSelfAdjoint (M s t)
   orthogonal :
      forall s t s' t',
         (s,t) != (s',t') ->
         M s t * M s' t' = 0
   complete :
      sum over s,t of M s t = 1

This structure lets the formalisation proceed while preserving the exact assumptions needed from the pointer readout.

theorem pointer_readout_complete :

theorem pointer_readout_orthogonal :
