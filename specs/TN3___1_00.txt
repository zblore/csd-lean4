1 Introduction

1.1 Purpose and Scope

The purpose of this note is to exhibit the standard von Neumann measurement construction for the two-qubit singlet
within the finite-dimensional geometric reconstruction developed in the surrounding papers. The aim is not to introduce
a new measurement Hamiltonian, nor to derive the Born rule independently. Rather, the aim is to show that the canonical
quantum-pointer measurement model translates directly into the present framework, with the usual branch separation,
pointer readout projectors, and singlet probabilities preserved without distortion.

The  system  consists  of  two  spin-  1  degrees  of  freedom  prepared  in  the  singlet  state
                                       2

                                          |-           =   1   (|+z |-z   -   |-z|+z) .
                                                          

                                                            2

The apparatus is represented by two pointer sectors, one coupled to each wing of the Bell experiment. In the finite-
dimensional version used here, these pointer sectors are treated as large truncations of continuous position-pointer
Hilbert spaces. The total system­apparatus Hilbert space is therefore finite-dimensional,

                                HS(NA) = C2  C2  KA(dA)  KB(dB),                      N = 4dAdB,

so that the corresponding projective state space is the compact Kähler manifold

                                                                CPN -1 .

The measurement dynamics is generated by the impulsive von Neumann Hamiltonian

                                        Ha,b = A( · a  I)  pA + B(I   · b)  pB,

where a, b  S2 are detector settings, pA and pB are pointer momentum operators, and free pointer evolution
is neglected during the readout interval. This is the usual idealised strong-measurement limit of von Neumann
measurement theory von Neumann [1955]. Through the Ashtekar­Schilling correspondence, the resulting Schrödinger
evolution is equivalently Hamiltonian flow on the joint projective manifold Ashtekar and Schilling [1999].

The note therefore has a deliberately limited scope. It establishes a system­apparatus realisation of the singlet
measurement inside the finite-dimensional geometric framework. It does not claim a first-principles derivation of the
singlet probability kernel, a novel alternative to the von Neumann measurement model, or a Bell-local hidden-variable
model.

1.3 Contribution of the Present Note

The contribution is one of compatibility and architectural closure. The standard measurement construction is embedded
into the finite-dimensional geometric setting without requiring an alternative measurement formalism. In particular:

      1. the two measurement couplings are represented by explicit setting-dependent Hamiltonian terms;
      2. the system­apparatus evolution separates the four pointer branches associated with outcomes s, t  {±1};
      3. pointer-sector projectors define the operational outcome sectors;
      4. the branch weights reduce to the standard projector expectations;
      5. direct summation yields E(a, b) = -a · b;
      6. marginal summation gives operational no-signalling.

No Fubini­Study Voronoi-volume construction is used. Outcome sectors are represented by pointer projectors of the

form                                  Ms(ta,b) = IAB  QsA  QtB ,

and their weights are computed using the operator-form probability rule

                                      Pst(a, b; T ) = T |Ms(ta,b)|T .

In the interpretive language of the wider framework, these pointer sectors correspond to measurement-context outcome
basins. The technical calculation in this note fixes their operational weights. The further ontological question of which
sector is realised in a single run is the basin-selection question of the broader programme. The present note assumes the
programme-level view that the ontic state is definite and that a realised measurement context selects one outcome sector,
while the sector weights are supplied by the finite-dimensional probability layer.

The construction is deterministic per realised context but not Bell-local. It does not define factorised outcome functions

                                      RA(, a), RB(, b).

Instead, the relevant outcome structure is context-indexed:

                                                  F (a,b) : (SNA) - {±1} × {±1}.

This is necessary for consistency with Bell-type constraints Bell [1964], Clauser et al. [1969]. The model is operationally
no-signalling because the marginals satisfy

Earlier formulations of this construction used projective-cell language. The present formulation instead uses pointer-

directly to the finite-dimensional Born-weight layer used upstream.

The main construction sets

7.5 Interpretation

The no-signalling result is operational. It concerns the observable statistics available to agents at either wing. It does
not require Bell-local outcome factorisation at the ontic level.

In the present construction, the measurement context is labelled by the pair
                                                                   (a, b),

and the branch-separated outcome sectors are defined relative to that context. Nevertheless, once the remote outcome is
ignored, the local marginal statistics are independent of the remote setting. Hence the model is context-indexed at the
level of outcome-sector structure but no-signalling at the level of operational probabilities.

This is the required compatibility condition for a deterministic single-outcome reconstruction of Bell-singlet statistics.

8 Bell Consistency

8.1 Context-Indexed Outcome Sectors

The construction above is deterministic relative to a realised measurement context. For each pair of detector settings

                                               (a, b),

the measurement Hamiltonian
                                                                    Ha,b

generates a branch-separated system­apparatus state, and the pointer projectors

                                               Ms(ta,b)

define the outcome sectors for that context.

In the ontic notation of the broader framework, this corresponds to a context-indexed outcome map

                                                  F (a,b) : (SNA) - {±1} × {±1}.
At the programme-level interpretive layer, each realised context is represented by a definite outcome-sector assignment

                                               F (a,b)(x) = (s, t),

for the underlying ontic state x. The present note does not independently derive this basin-selection map; it constructs
the corresponding system­apparatus pointer sectors and their operational weights.

The dependence on the full context (a, b) is essential. The model does not assign outcomes through context-independent
local functions. Instead, each experimental arrangement determines its own system­apparatus Hamiltonian and its own
pointer-sector decomposition.

This is the standard structure of a contextual measurement model. Outcomes are definite within a performed context,

9.3 Relation to Bohmian Branch Selection

The construction has a clear structural parallel with Bohmian mechanics applied to a Bell-singlet measurement Bohm

Born probabilities for the measurement statistics.

In Bohmian mechanics, the actual configuration of the system and apparatus lies in one branch of the post-measurement

the present framework, the analogous statement is that the ontic state lies in one realised outcome sector associated
with the measurement context. The operator-form weights determine the preparation-relative sector weights, while the
programme-level ontology supplies single-outcome definiteness.

The distinction is that the present framework expresses the system­apparatus dynamics through symplectic Hamiltonian
flow on projective state space in the geometric quantum mechanics formulation, with the wider ontic interpretation
organised through the projection

present technical note.

For the purpose of this note, the important point is narrower: the standard branch-selection structure of a quantum-pointer
measurement is compatible with the finite-dimensional geometric architecture.

9.4 Limitations and Non-Claims

Several limitations should be stated explicitly.

First, the construction does not derive the Born rule. The probability rule is inherited from the upstream finite-
dimensional Born-weight layer. The present note shows how those probabilities arise as pointer-sector weights in a
standard system­apparatus model.

Second, the construction does not derive the singlet probability kernel from first principles. The expression

is obtained from the usual singlet projector calculation, as already used in Technical Note 2.

Third, the construction is not Bell-local. It does not define factorised outcome functions

                                                  A(a, ), B(b, ).

Instead, it defines outcome sectors for each realised measurement context (a, b). This is necessary for compatibility
with Bell-type constraints.

Fourth, the pointer sectors are finite-dimensional truncations. The continuous pointer model is treated only as a
controlled comparison limit. Exact statements are made at fixed finite truncation.

Fifth, the note does not provide a general classification of all physically realisable measurement partitions. It supplies
one explicit canonical construction: the von Neumann quantum-pointer measurement model for the two-qubit singlet.

Sixth, the note does not by itself solve the full measurement problem. It shows how standard branch separation and
pointer readout are represented in the finite-dimensional geometric architecture. The deeper ontological account of
single-outcome branch selection belongs to the wider framework.
